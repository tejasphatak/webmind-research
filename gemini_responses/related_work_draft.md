### 2. Related Work

Our work intersects several distinct areas of machine learning systems, ranging from distributed inference architectures to tensor decomposition and verifiable computation. 

**Distributed Inference Systems**
Decentralized and swarm-based inference systems have emerged as a democratic alternative to centralized, monolithic cluster deployments. Petals [Borzunov et al., 2023] is the closest architectural predecessor to our work, enabling collaborative inference of large language models over the internet via pipeline parallelism. Petals, alongside underlying distributed hash table (DHT) and networking frameworks like Hivemind, successfully abstracts the routing of requests across heterogeneous consumer GPUs. However, these systems currently rely on naive activation transport, transmitting raw FP16 or BF16 hidden states between nodes. As batch sizes or model dimensions scale, this uncompressed transport rapidly saturates consumer-grade internet bandwidth, shifting the bottleneck from compute to communication. Our work directly addresses this transport bottleneck.

**Activation Compression and Quantization**
Quantization is the standard approach to reducing LLM memory and compute requirements. Weight quantization methods such as GPTQ [Frantar et al., 2022] and AWQ [Lin et al., 2023] are highly effective at reducing the memory footprint of model parameters, but they are orthogonal to our problem, as they do not compress the intermediate activations passed between pipeline stages. 

For activations, LLM.int8! [Dettmers et al., 2022] introduced outlier-aware quantization to enable 8-bit matrix multiplications without performance degradation, while SmoothQuant [Xiao et al., 2023] mathematically migrates the difficulty of quantization from the activation space to the weight space. However, these methods are designed strictly to optimize intra-node compute and memory bandwidth. They are not designed for pipeline-parallel *transport* over constrained networks. While 8-bit quantization offers a theoretical 2x reduction in payload size, it falls drastically short of the compression ratios required for efficient decentralized inference.

**Low-Rank Structure in Transformers**
Our approach is heavily motivated by findings regarding the intrinsic dimensionality of neural network representations. Ethayarajh [2019] demonstrated that contextual representations in Transformers are highly anisotropic, occupying a narrow cone in the vector space rather than being uniformly distributed. Aghajanyan et al. [2020] further showed that the intrinsic dimensionality of the objective landscape during fine-tuning is remarkably low. This insight was famously operationalized by LoRA [Hu et al., 2021], which exploits low-rank structures to compress weight updates. We extend this foundational intuition from weights and gradients to the forward-pass residual stream: if the learned transformations are low-rank, the intermediate activations flowing through the pipeline must also exhibit extreme low-rank structure. 

**Tensor Decomposition for Communication**
The use of tensor decomposition to alleviate communication bottlenecks is well-established in distributed *training*. Techniques like PowerSGD [Vogels et al., 2019] utilize low-rank approximations to compress gradients before synchronization across data-parallel workers. In the realm of activations, techniques like activation checkpointing [Chen et al., 2016] discard and recompute forward-pass tensors to save memory, but this does not reduce node-to-node communication. Applying tensor decomposition specifically to forward-pass activations for pipeline-parallel transport remains largely unexplored. In traditional datacenter deployments, intra-node bandwidth (via NVLink or PCIe) is rarely the primary bottleneck, rendering activation compression unnecessary. In decentralized settings, however, it is the critical limiting factor.

**Verifiable Computation**
A core challenge in decentralized inference is ensuring that untrusted nodes execute the model faithfully. Cryptographic approaches like Zero-Knowledge Machine Learning (ZKML) (e.g., EZKL) provide mathematically rigorous proofs of execution but incur massive computational overheads that are currently prohibitive for LLMs. Optimistic approaches, such as opML [Zheng et al., 2024], rely on fraud proofs and economic game theory, which introduce latency via dispute periods. Earlier works like SafetyNets [Garg et al., 2017] applied Freivalds' algorithm for verifiable execution of deep neural networks. Our architecture introduces a middle ground: lightweight probabilistic verification. By decoupling the activation into a dense carrier and a sparse residual, our protocol enables downstream nodes to perform rapid, probabilistic parity checks on the payload without the prohibitive overhead of cryptographic proofs.

**Positioning of Our Work**
We do not claim mathematical novelty in applying Principal Component Analysis (PCA) or low-rank approximations to tensors; these are fundamental statistical techniques. Rather, our contribution is the introduction of the *Carrier-Payload protocol*—a novel application of these techniques specifically engineered for the wire transport of activations in decentralized LLM routing. By splitting the activation into a low-rank carrier and a sparse residual, we demonstrate that the effective dimensionality of the residual stream is shockingly low during inference. Empirically, we achieve a 22x compression ratio with a negligible Kullback-Leibler divergence (KL=0.023) on Gemma 3 1B IT. This proves that the vast majority of data transmitted in current decentralized systems like Petals is redundant, and that extreme activation compression can resolve the decentralized communication bottleneck without sacrificing generation quality.
